Project Overview

Understanding the COMPAS algorithm and its implications

What is COMPAS?

COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions) is a case management and decision support tool developed by Northpointe (now Equivant) used by U.S. courts to assess the likelihood of a defendant becoming a recidivist.

The algorithm uses a proprietary method to assign scores that predict the risk of recidivism, which has been used to inform decisions about bail, sentencing, and parole.

Audit Objectives

This comprehensive audit evaluates the COMPAS algorithm against Google's AI Principles to assess its fairness, accountability, and transparency.

The analysis includes statistical evaluation of the algorithm's predictive performance across different demographic groups and an assessment of its compliance with ethical AI principles.

  • Evaluate fairness across racial and gender groups
  • Assess transparency and interpretability
  • Review accountability measures
  • Provide recommendations for improvement

Methodology

Our approach to evaluating the COMPAS algorithm

Data Collection

Gathered COMPAS recidivism risk scores and demographic data from Broward County, Florida, including over 7,000 criminal defendants from 2013-2014.

Statistical Analysis

Performed comprehensive analysis including confusion matrices, ROC curves, and fairness metrics to evaluate predictive performance across demographic groups.

Principle Evaluation

Assessed the algorithm against each of Google's AI Principles using both quantitative metrics and qualitative analysis.

Google's AI Principles

Evaluating COMPAS against ethical AI guidelines

Be Socially Beneficial

AI should benefit society and avoid creating or reinforcing unfair bias. The COMPAS algorithm's impact on marginalized communities raises concerns about its social benefit.

Avoid Creating or Reinforcing Unfair Bias

AI systems should be designed to avoid unfair bias. Our analysis shows significant racial disparities in COMPAS's false positive rates.

Be Built and Tested for Safety

AI systems should be robustly tested to ensure safety. COMPAS's proprietary nature makes independent safety verification difficult.

Be Accountable to People

AI systems should include accountability mechanisms. COMPAS lacks transparency and clear accountability pathways.

Incorporate Privacy Design Principles

AI systems should incorporate privacy protections. COMPAS uses sensitive personal data with unclear privacy safeguards.

Uphold High Standards of Scientific Excellence

AI should uphold scientific excellence. COMPAS's proprietary algorithm limits scientific scrutiny and peer review.

Key Findings

Results from our comprehensive audit of the COMPAS algorithm

1

Racial Disparities in False Positives

Black defendants were almost twice as likely to be misclassified as higher risk compared to white defendants. The false positive rate was 45% for Black defendants versus 23% for white defendants.

2

Accuracy Discrepancies

Overall predictive accuracy was similar across racial groups (around 60-70%), but the distribution of errors showed significant racial disparities.

3

Lack of Transparency

The proprietary nature of the COMPAS algorithm makes it impossible for defendants, judges, or the public to understand how risk scores are calculated.

4

Limited Accountability

No clear process exists for challenging or appealing COMPAS risk scores, and Northpointe (Equivant) accepts limited responsibility for algorithmic errors.

5

Inadequate Validation

Independent validation of the algorithm's fairness and accuracy has been limited, and the validation performed by Northpointe has not been fully disclosed.

6

Gender Biases

While less pronounced than racial disparities, the algorithm also shows differences in error rates between male and female defendants.

7

Contextual Limitations

The algorithm does not account for contextual factors such as policing patterns, socioeconomic conditions, or regional variations that might affect recidivism rates.

Recommendations

Actions to improve fairness, accountability, and transparency

Algorithmic Transparency

Make the COMPAS algorithm open source or provide detailed documentation of its methodology, features, and weighting system to enable independent auditing.

Regular Bias Audits

Implement mandatory, regular third-party audits to assess and mitigate biases across different demographic groups, with results made publicly available.

Human Oversight

Ensure that COMPAS scores are used as one input among many in decision-making, with human judgment serving as the final arbiter.

Clear Documentation

Provide clear documentation to users about the limitations of the algorithm, its error rates, and appropriate contexts for use.

Appeal Process

Establish a clear process for defendants to challenge their risk scores and receive explanations of how their scores were calculated.

Continuous Monitoring

Implement ongoing monitoring of algorithm performance across different jurisdictions and demographic groups to identify and address disparities.
